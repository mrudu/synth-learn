In this example, we consider a system that is composed of three tower cranes, where each crane is operated through a control panel. The safety of the entire system relies on a central controller that will arbitrate the movements of the tower cranes to avoid collisions. This is done by ensuring that no two cranes enter the exclusion sections at the same time (see ExSect1 and ExSect2 in Fig. 1). Additionally, the central controller will be fair to the cranes in the sense that it will not prevent the cranes to enter those exclusion sections indefinitely, in order to allow the cranes to always eventually move from any position x to another position.

Representation of Environment/System interaction in atomic propoisitons

The environment here represents the two cranes. The following are the atomic propositions of the environment:

  1. Request Access: r(i)

    Request made by crane i to enter the critical section

  2. Release Access: l(i)

    Release signal sent by crane i when it exits the critical section

The system here is the arbiter and the atomic propositions of the outputs are as follows:
  1. Grant Access: g(i)

    Grant access to the critical section given to crane i.

Design Specifications/Requirements

To synthesize an efficient arbiter, we must express the precise design specifications. We can express these specifications formally in Linear Temporal Logic (LTL). We classify the design specifications as Safety and Functionality/Fairness.

Safety Specifications
  1. Mutual Exclusion Condition:

    We must ensure that no two cranes can access the critical section at the same time. We can express in LTL as:

    G (!g1 & !g2)

  2. Release Holds Grant:

    We must ensure that once the crane has access to the critical section, then it must retain its acess until it chooses release the access to the critical section. We can express in LTL as:

    G (g(i) => (g(i) U l(i)))

Functionality Specifications
  1. Fairness:

    The arbitrer must ensure fairness, i.e., every request made by any crane must be eventually granted. We can express in LTL as:

    G (r(i) -> F g(i))
