In this example, we consider an elevator system that traverses between two floors. Each floor has a button that can be pressed to call the elevator to that floor. The movement of the elevator is controlled by the arbiter that ensures "liveness" property, i.e., every floor button pressed implies elevator will eventually "reach" the floor and "serve" that floor.

Representation of Environment/System interaction in atomic propoisitons

The environment here represents the user. The following are the atomic propositions of the environment:

  1. Floor Button: b(i)

    Button of Floor i pressed by user to request elevator service on Floor i

The system here is the elevator controllre and the atomic propositions of the outputs are as follows:
  1. Floor Position: f(i)

    Represents that elevator is on floor i.

  2. Serve Floor: serve

    Represents that elevator doors are open on floor i.

Design Specifications/Requirements

To synthesize an efficient controller, we must express the precise design specifications. We can express these specifications formally in Linear Temporal Logic (LTL). We classify the design specifications as Safety/Sanity and Functionality/Fairness.

Safety/Sanity Specifications
  1. Elevator Traversal Condition:

    We must ensure that the elevator does not change floors when the doors are open:

    G ((fi & serve) => X (f(i)))

  2. Sanity Check:

    We must ensure the controller restricts the lift to be exactly at one floor:

    G ((f(0) & !f(1)) | (!f(0) & f(1)))

Functionality Specifications
  1. Fairness:

    The controller must ensure fairness, i.e., every request (button press) made by the user must be eventually satisfied. We can express in LTL as:

    G (b(i) -> F (f(i) & serve))
